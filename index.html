Homelessness Twitter analysis

A twitter analysis on the conversation around homelessness over the Christmas period of 2020.

By Robyn Hamilton January 11th, 2021

Homelessness has been, and remains a constant global problem in society. I was particularly drawn to explore the topic as the issue of homelessness has persistently dominated news headlines in my home country of Ireland for the better part of the last decade, in response to its ongoing housing crisis.

Observing the problem on a larger scale, if we look at the global search trends for the last five years, we can see a pattern. As temperatures fall going into the winter months of November, December and January, we can see repeated spikes in search volume for the term “homeless” over these months.

December of course, also sees the Christmas period, which (in Christian countries at least) is traditionally celebrated as a time of charity, giving and thinking of more vulnerable and less fortunate members of society.

Typically, homelessness charities and other organisations who help the homeless launch campaigns around the Christmas period to appeal to this seasonal charitable sensibility by reminding the public of those battling the elements without shelter in the colder weather.

After a historically turbulent year, where the world not only battled an unprecedented global pandemic but also bore witness to many societal upheavals including, but not limited to the Black Lives Matter movement, a chaotic US election and Brexit finally coming into effect; I was interested to explore whether these issues have had an effect on the conversation around homelessness.

Research

From a dataset of 53,763 tweets scraped from Twitter, this report explore what was posted, who was posting and what was reshared. The analysis will answer the following questions:

In what contexts was homelessness discussed? Can we see patterns to the types of tweets that were posted and retweeted? Does the sentiment of the discussion vary from place to place?

Additionally, the analysis will examine if and how broad current events affected the conversation around homelessness over the Christmas period.

Research methodology

After researching some popular terms used around the topic of homelessness on Twitter, using Python code, I scraped tweets containing the following hashtags "#homelessness”, “#homeless”, “#sleepingrough”, “#roughsleeping”, “#endhomelessness”, “#housingfirst”, as well as the general terms (not containing a hashtag) “homeless” and “homelessness” during hourly slots between the dates of 19/12/2020 and 03/01/2021 to cover the lead up to, and days following the Christmas period.

During a total of 16.5 hours over these dates, I collected 53,763 tweets from 46,058 unique users. About 35% of these tweets were original. The remainder were retweets, with approximately 13 tweets in 20 being retweets.

Bot analysis

Before doing any analysis on the most prolific tweeters of the dataset, I assessed the top 20 accounts to see if I could weed out any potential bots. I used a combination of two bot detection tools, Botomoter and Botsight to inform my own critical judgement of each account.

Of the top 20 accounts, I judged eight of them to be definite bots, three accounts had been suspended since the data was collected and a further two accounts lay on the line, as I could not be fully sure that they were bots or genuine humans. That left just six accounts out of the original top 20, that I determined to be genuine accounts.

What kind of accounts feature in the top ten?

The top ten accounts consists of a mixture of individuals and homelessness aid organisations. The types of tweets that these accounts made fall into three main categories:

  1. Criticism of primarily US government officials/policies perceived to lead to widespread homelessness (@joepdr, @rwwonwheels)
  2. Homelessness charities/individuals raising awareness of homelessness and/or seeking donations (@beanies_masato, @NatlHCHCouncil, @Foxography2, @KatieLeague, @invisiblepeople).
  3. Homeless/ex-homeless individuals criticising homelessness organisations in the US and UK (@Homeless_Info, @ashleydeangelo1).

There was one outlier in the top ten, @jmuhj1 who tweeted exclusively about cats and cat welfare.

How often were people tweeting?

The vast majority of accounts did not tweet nearly as many times as the top ten tweeters. The 46,058 unique users averaged 1.17 tweets or retweets over the 16.5 tracked hours over the 15 day period.

Insert tweet volumes bar chart

Users were much more likely to retweet rather than write original tweets, given that almost 65% of the total number of tweets were retweets. It is important not to overlook the relevance of retweets in a dataset. Although they may not communicate original thoughts and opinions, they are an important barometer to gauge the popularity of certain sentiments around a given topic.

In ‘The Why We Retweet Scale’ Majmundar et al. posit that retweets can communicate many different intentions, which can be grouped into four general category definitions. These are as follows:

  1. Show approval, "Show support to the tweeter"
  2. Argue, "To argue against a tweet that I disagree with"
  3. Gain attention, "Add followers or gain attention"
  4. Entertain, "Create humor/amusement"
(Majmundar et al., 2018)

Where were people tweeting from?

65% of the total tweets listed a location in the user’s profile bio. Unfortunately however, location data is set by the user and therefore must be taken with a grain of salt.

This is because, firstly, users are free to set their location to anything they want (including fictional places or text that doesn’t denote a location at all). Secondly, even if a user does enter a real location, it might not necessarily reflect where the user is actually tweeting from.

What were people saying?

Insert word cloud

The word cloud above shows the most popular words and phrases that appeared in the dataset. This illustrates the nature of the discussion around homelessness. I made it using two online word cloud generating tools.

First I used MonkeyLearn to find out what were the most popular phrases (as opposed to singular words) and then I took this data and used it produce a customised word cloud using Word Art.

Additionally, using the word frequency data gathered using these tools, I analysed both unique tweets and retweets to find some of the most popular topics tweeted about in relation to homelessness.

I considered performing this analysis by isolating popular hashtags in the data but discovered that only 16.5% of tweeters used one or more hashtag (#) symbols. As such, instead I analysed the tweet text data by isolating tweets containing the word ‘homeless’ (without the # prefix) in conjunction with ten popular topics that appeared a significant amount in the data.

Insert frequency of topics line chart

The most popular topic by far was Covid/pandemic with tweeters primarily expressing sentiments on how Covid19 has negatively impacted the problem of homelessness. Criticism of governmental policies was the top of the discussion list, but the dataset also showed a wide range of opinions including but not limited to how the pandemic has led to:

  1. More people being homeless/facing homelessness now due to mass unemployment.
  2. Homeless people being unable to or having reduced access to public buildings.
  3. Homeless shelters being unable to take in homeless individuals.
  4. Increased need for support and donations to homelessness charities.
  5. Increased need for awareness of homeless people’s welfare in the face of the challenges that Covid restrictions present.

An outlying opinion that proved to be surprisingly popular came through in a number of tweets. It questioned whether the pandemic is real or a hoax. These tweets used the homeless community as the strawman for their argument, suggesting that the homeless mortality rate would be far higher if we were experiencing a genuine pandemic. For example:

“@Braveheart_USA: If this was a real pandemic, the homeless population would be decimated and bodies would be everywhere in California.... Let that sink in” (retweeted 34 times).

The most retweeted English language tweet of the entire dataset, mentioned the second most popular topic in relation to homelessness – Trump.
