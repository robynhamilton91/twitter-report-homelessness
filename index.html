Will it be lonely this Christmas?

With new COVID-19 cases finally falling, should Ireland suspend air travel this December?

By Robyn Hamilton November 12th, 2020

The Republic of Ireland, now halfway through a second national lockdown, earlier this month saw its R number drop below 1. This means that the number of people one infected person passes the virus to is now less than one, on average.

Where most other European nations are seeing an upward trajectory in terms of new cases, Ireland managed to turn its status around, just two weeks into a six-week lockdown. An R number below one indicates that the disease will eventually stop spreading, as not enough new people are being infected to sustain the outbreak.

It’s promising news for many hopeful families anticipating the homecoming of overseas loved ones this December, but is a low R number now, enough to save Christmas?

When lockdown is lifted on December 1, should Ireland keep its airports open and allow for an influx of thousands of potentially infected visitors? Let’s examine the facts.

The current COVID-19 landscape in Ireland

Right now, according to the EU’s COVID-19 traffic-light travel system (newly adopted in the Republic) Ireland is red. This means that the country’s 14-day incidence rate is above 150 per 100,000, and the test positivity rate is higher than 4%.

What does that mean? Well, if you want to travel in or out of the country, you’ll be expected to restrict your movement for 14 days, unless you have the results of a negative PCR test, received no longer than three days before your arrival.

October saw cases at an all-time high and it was only at the end of the month, for the first time in four weeks, that the number of new confirmed COVID-19 cases for the whole country fell below 5,000 to 3,881, marking a considerable decrease of 2,546 from the previous week.

Dublin, the capital, by Halloween accounted for the lion’s share of all cases, seeing its seventh week in a row of more than 1,000 cases. Though its decline in new cases over the last couple of weeks hasn’t been as dramatic as the rest of the country’s, nevertheless we can see significant progress, no doubt linked the restrictive measures of lockdown 2.

We can see that the mortality rate (per 1,000 confirmed cases) has decreased by a massive 95.9% since its peak of 74 per 1,000 last April, despite the fact that the case numbers have increased by 92.2% over the same period.

The numbers would indicate that the health service’s abilities to understand and cope with the effects of the disease have vastly improved, with decreasing hospitalisation and ICU admission rates also falling to tell a similar story.

So, if the R number is below 1, Lockdown 2 is helping to keep cases down, as well as evidence pointing to health services being better equipped to deal with the infected; is it all enough to have a semi-normal Christmas?

What do the experts say?

To date, the Irish government along with the National Public Health Emergency Team (NPHET) have been very cautious in their approach to offering travel advice around the Christmas period.

Speaking at a recent Oireachtas Transport and Communication Networks meeting, Chief Medical Officer (and Chair of NPHET) Dr Tony Holohan, first stressed that it’s still too early to offer concrete advice for travel into the country over the Christmas period next month.

He acknowledged the recent progress made in lowering the R number but also cautioned that even if Ireland manages to decrease its cases significantly, virus rates would have to be at similarly low levels both at home and abroad to mitigate the risks associated with air travel.

“International travel is not safe and movement of people around areas with high incidence of this disease, particularly in Europe and North America, in and out of this country, is not safe,” he told the assembled TDs and senators.

Dr Holohan also expressed concerns about any “arrangements that we put in place over the course of the month of December that lead to very significant importation of the disease or importation of the disease that we are not able to detect and prevent.”

Though the second quarter of 2020 saw just 164,400 passengers pass through the five main Irish airports (a decrease of 98.4% over the same period in 2019), Holohan said he would “absolutely” and “totally” deny any insinuations that he is being alarmist, stating that the risks around travel are very substantial.

Speaking to Newstalk Breakfast, immunology expert Professor Kingston Mills asserted similar thinking on the issue, noting that he found it “disheartening” that Christmas is being used as the carrot to get numbers down.

"The idea that we should all be locked up for five weeks to save Christmas is ridiculous.

"It's more important to get the pandemic under control, get the economy back to working again, and get normal life back again.

"If we allow the numbers to resurrect in mid-December, were going to be back to square one.
